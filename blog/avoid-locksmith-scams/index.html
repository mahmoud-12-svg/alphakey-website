How to Avoid
Locksmith Scams

Locked out is a stressful moment, and that's exactly when scammers try to take advantage. Here's what to watch for.

Locksmith scams follow a predictable pattern: a rock-bottom quote gets you to call, then the price jumps dramatically once someone shows up. Knowing the pattern ahead of time is the best protection.

Warning Signs

  • Vague pricing over the phone. A legitimate locksmith can give you a realistic price range before arriving. If they refuse to give any estimate, be cautious.
  • Unmarked vehicles or no uniform. Professional locksmiths typically arrive in a marked vehicle and identifiable uniform.
  • Reaching for a drill immediately. A qualified locksmith can pick most locks. Drilling should be a last resort, not the first move, and should be explained to you first.
  • Refusing to provide ID or licensing. You should be able to ask for identification or a license number without pushback.
  • The price changes dramatically on arrival. Some scammers quote a low price to get you to agree, then claim the job is "more complicated" once they're on-site and demand far more.
  • Cash-only, no invoice. A legitimate business will give you an itemized invoice for the work performed.

How to Protect Yourself

  • Search for the business by name (not just the phone number in an ad) and check reviews before you call, when you have time to do so.
  • Ask for the total price to be confirmed again once the technician arrives, before any work starts.
  • Get the technician's name and the company name up front.
  • If the price jumps significantly with no clear explanation, you're allowed to decline and call someone else.
